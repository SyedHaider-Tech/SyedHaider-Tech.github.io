Techno-Economic Analysis 10 min read November 2025

The hidden cost curves of green
hydrogen at scale.

Most LCOH models published in 2023–25 converge on the same headline number — but they diverge wildly on three structural factors that dominate first-of-a-kind plant economics. Here's what the spreadsheets miss.

The convergence problem

If you read ten different green-hydrogen techno-economic studies published in the last two years, you'll notice something curious. The headline LCOH numbers cluster tightly — typically USD 3–5 per kilogram by 2030 — but the underlying assumptions span almost an order of magnitude on capex, capacity factor, and electrolyzer lifetime. The convergence isn't real; it's an artifact of analysts anchoring on each other's published numbers.

This matters because decisions are being made — about offtake contracts, sovereign hydrogen strategies, and multi-billion-dollar plant investments — on the basis of numbers that look more certain than they are. Let me walk through the three factors that I think dominate, and that most published TEAs handle poorly.

Factor 1 — Stack degradation under realistic duty cycles

Almost every published model assumes electrolyzer stack lifetime as a static number: 80,000 hours, or 10 years, often with a one-time replacement assumption. The reality is more brutal.

Stacks degrade as a function of:

  • Cycling — startups, shutdowns, and load-following from renewable input.
  • Current density — running hotter to produce more per unit capex accelerates membrane and electrode wear.
  • Feed-water purity — sub-spec water silently shortens life in ways that only show up in year three.

A plant designed for 90% capacity factor on grid power behaves very differently from one designed for 35% capacity factor on a solar feedstock. The same capex, the same nameplate output, but the second plant replaces stacks roughly twice as often.

The error here isn't that analysts ignore degradation. It's that they treat it as a single number — when it's actually a function of how the plant is run.

Factor 2 — Balance-of-plant cost trajectories

The electrolyzer stack itself is the visible cost — the part everyone benchmarks and tracks. But for a complete green-hydrogen facility, the stack is typically only 25–35% of total installed capex. The rest is power electronics, water treatment, gas conditioning, compression, storage, civil works, and integration.

These costs are not falling at the same rate as the stack. Power electronics scale roughly with grid-scale inverter learning curves (slow). Civil works don't scale at all. Compression and storage costs are dominated by steel, copper, and labor — all of which are inflating, not deflating, in most jurisdictions in 2025.

When you decompose published cost-down trajectories, the often-cited "stack costs falling 50% by 2030" translates to a much more modest ~20–25% reduction in total installed plant cost. That's the number that should be in the LCOH model.

Factor 3 — First-of-a-kind premiums

Almost no published TEA models a first-of-a-kind (FOAK) plant honestly. They model an nth-of-a-kind plant — one where the supply chain is mature, EPC contractors have built five of these, and bankability is solved.

FOAK plants pay a premium of 30–60% on EPC, 50–100% on contingency, and 100–300 basis points on cost of capital. That premium doesn't disappear in a model; it disappears in the assumption that the model is describing a hypothetical future plant rather than the actual investment decision in front of you.

What this means for decisions

If you're evaluating a green-hydrogen investment, building a national strategy, or signing an offtake contract, the published LCOH numbers are a starting point — not an endpoint. Three questions to ask:

  1. Does the model treat stack lifetime as a function of duty cycle, or as a static number?
  2. Does it disaggregate installed capex into stack vs. balance-of-plant, with appropriate learning curves on each?
  3. Does it explicitly distinguish FOAK from NOAK economics?

If the answer to any of these is no, the LCOH number is at least 30–50% too low. That's the gap between an attractive investment and an obvious one.
